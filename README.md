***This is the repository for the paper titled "Which Metrics Transfer? Generalizing Expressive Range Analysis Across Games and Generators in GVGAI" by Dr. Britton Horn, Justin McDuffey, and Justin Rodriguez. This research was conducted as a part of Trinity University's 2026 Summer Undergraduate Research Fellowship, and was funded by the Murchison and McNair Research Fellowships. A brief summary of the work conducted is provided below.***
## Problem Statement:
Despite the wide use of Procedural Content Generation (PCG) in game design and other applications, evaluating the output of generators remains a persistent challenge. Our research applies 31 metrics drawn from existing literature to levels generated by ten generators across ten games in the GVGAI Framework. Our objective is to determine which of these metrics are useful for assessing level quality, and whether they can distinguish between generator outputs. We generate 1,000 levels per game for the eight conventional generators, and 100 levels for the two LLM-based generators due to API costs. We then utilize Expressive Range Analysis (ERA) to visualize and compare evaluated metrics for each generator, focusing on transferability and independence. This work aims to advance the understanding of metric-based evaluation in procedural content generation through a broad comparison of metrics across a diverse set of games.
## Methodology:
For this study, we choose a diverse suite of ten games both representative of the GVGAI framework and generalizable to other applications of PCG. This list encompasses puzzle games, platformers, dungeon crawlers, consisting of the GVGAI implementations of Aliens, Artillery, Asteroids, Dungeon, Frogs, Mario, Roguelike, Sokoban, Tower Defense, and Zelda. Next, we conduct a survey of existing PCG/Game AI literature to gather over 40 metrics across 22 different papers, yielding a total of 31 metrics that could be implemented in the GVGAI framework.
![[image_2.png]]
We analyze a total of ten generators. The Constructive, Random, and Genetic level generators are native to the GVGAI framework, and reflect more traditional PCG methods. Aside from small performance fixes for genetic generator, these generators were implemented largely out of the box. The four Sturgeon generator variations each perform Wave Function Collapse (WFC) at a separate 2D N-gram resolution (1x1, 2x2, 3x3 and 4x4), while the three LLM based generators (Zero-Shot Gemini, Zero-Shot Claude, and Iterative Claude) prompt an AI model to design a level.
## Results:
To elucidate the relationships between metrics among thousands of generated levels, we utilize Expressive Range Analysis (ERA) to construct visually intuitive representations of a generator's output space. Shown below is an ERA chart mapping Static Path Length to Flood Reachability for levels produced by the Constructive generator. Below it is the corresponding density estimation chart, which provides an even more direct view into the the distribution of path length and reachability in generated levels.

In interpreting our results, we put particular emphasis on determining which metrics show similar behavior across games. In the figures shown below, a high degree of clustering between games implies transferability, while discrete groupings of games indicate game independence. In the leftmost chart below, we see a great degree of overlap (transferability) between games, while for the chart on the right, the opposite is apparent.

![[image_5.png|697]]
We can also analyze the expressive range of a generator along a single dimension,  allowing us to interpret metrics without interference from a second axis. Just like in the examples above, clustering, expressed in a histogram as tall, prominent peaks, indicates high metric transferability. Additionally, smooth slopes signal gradients in generator expression, whereas abrupt cliffs may be artifacts of strict generator constraints.
![[image_6.png]]
In addition to transferability, we take into account which metrics exhibit significant statistical correlations. If two metrics are useful in discriminating a certain quality, but highly correlated with another, then there is a reduced benefit in implementing both of them compared to implementing another useful, but orthogonal metric. For example, across practically all games and generators, 1D N-Gram similarity is highly correlated with 2D N-Gram similarity, with an R² value of .60, much greater than the average R² value of .17 between all metrics, signaling that the development and implementation of both of these metrics may not be necessary.
![[image_7.png|697]]
Our results also reveal several metric pairs with more complex relationships, such as Density and Shannon Entropy, which form a horizontal crescent, suggesting a strong nonlinear interrelation.

## Takeaways:
- High metric transferability between games is rare
- Small amount of metrics correlate (3% metric pairs have an R² above .5).
- Generators produce distinct metric signatures.
- Similar games generally overlap in metrics more than dissimilar games.
- Visualizing such high-dimensional data is inherently constrained, and often requires unconventional techniques.
